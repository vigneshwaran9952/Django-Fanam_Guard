## Regulatory Comparison with India

### Common Requirements (As a summary)
Regulated entities must secure prior authorization for financial activities, maintain robust capital and liquidity buffers, and adhere to sound governance principles, including board oversight, senior management accountability, and fit-and-proper standards. Institutions must manage financial crime risks through AML/KYC and sanctions compliance, prevent fraud, and ensure market integrity. Technology risks, operational resilience, data protection, and outsourcing require comprehensive frameworks. Customer protection, complaints handling, and financial inclusion are mandated. Entities must implement enterprise-wide risk management, internal controls, and audit functions, supported by stress testing and recovery planning. Regulatory reporting, public disclosures, and engagement with supervisory reviews are obligatory, with enforcement powers exercised for non-compliance and processes for orderly market exit and structural changes. ESG, climate risk, crypto-assets, and RegTech integration are also subject to regulatory oversight.

### Comparison with India (As a summary)
India's regulatory framework specifies that NBFC registration requires a certificate when financial activity constitutes the principal business, defines regulated activities based on functional tests, and prohibits unauthorized acceptance of public deposits. The framework mandates data localization for customer data, specifies oversight for Digital Lending Service Providers, requires IRDAI approval for Corporate Agent Licenses, and provides for external escalation of complaints to the RBI Ombudsman Scheme. India's framework also explicitly promotes RegTech adoption and SupTech initiatives.

## Regulatory Comparison with United States

### Common Requirements (As a summary)
Institutions must obtain prior authorization for banking and regulated financial activities, with unauthorized activity prohibited, and financial activities are subject to regulation, where offering services without authorization constitutes a breach. Minimum capital and prudential buffers must be maintained, and capital adequacy is supervised. Liquidity risk must be managed, and adequate liquidity buffers maintained to withstand stress scenarios, with funding diversification required. Changes in ownership, control, and foreign ownership require regulatory approval. Financial groups are subject to consolidated supervision, and boards are responsible for strategy, risk oversight, and regulatory compliance. Senior management are individually accountable, and directors and senior officers must meet integrity and competence standards. Compensation structures must not encourage excessive risk-taking. Institutions must promote ethical conduct and manage conduct risk. Mandatory AML frameworks require compliance. Compliance with sanctions regimes is mandatory, and institutions must detect and prevent fraud. Market abuse prohibitions and insider trading prohibitions apply to securities and investment activities. Firms must enable reporting of misconduct. Institutions must manage cybersecurity risks and ensure continuity of critical services through business continuity planning. Customer/personal data must be protected. Outsourcing requires risk assessment and oversight. AI and models must be governed and risk-managed. Open banking is subject to regulatory frameworks, and banking operations require regulatory supervision. Payment services are regulated, and e-money is treated as stored value. Consumer credit products are regulated, and investment services require investor protection controls. Insurance distribution is regulated. Cross-border services are subject to regulatory controls. Consumer protection and customer fairness are regulatory expectations, and institutions must manage complaints effectively. Institutions must support social objectives, and vulnerable customers must be protected. Enterprise-wide risk frameworks covering all material risks are mandatory, and independent compliance functions and internal controls are mandatory. Internal audit must provide independent assurance over controls. Stress testing assesses resilience to adverse conditions, and institutions must plan for orderly resolution. Accurate and timely reporting is required for supervision, and financial statements must be audited and accurately reported. Banks must disclose risk and capital information. Regulators conduct supervision. Firms must track and implement regulatory changes. Regulatory communications require prompt action. Breaches or non-compliance may result in administrative or criminal penalties/sanctions. Orderly exit must protect customers and stability. Structural changes and M&A transactions require regulatory approval. ESG and sustainable finance considerations are subject to regulatory expectations/supervision. Climate risk is treated as financial risk. Crypto activities are regulated/supervised. Regulators encourage technology-enabled compliance/supervision.

### Comparison with United States (As a summary)
In the United States, federal or state charters are required for deposit-taking or core banking activities, with regulators assessing safety, soundness, governance, and business plans. Specific activities such as deposit-taking, lending, payments, custody, and trust are regulated, and activities outside the perimeter may trigger enforcement if mischaracterized. Capital rules vary by institution size and complexity, and large banks are subject to enhanced liquidity standards. Foreign investment in U.S. banks is closely supervised, and controllers must meet fitness standards. Activities of bank holding companies must be permissible and properly structured, with non-bank activities restricted. Boards are responsible for safety and soundness, and governance must ensure independent challenge, with regulators assessing board effectiveness. Accountability is enforced through individual responsibility, enforcement actions, and safety and soundness standards. Regulators may object to appointments, and ongoing suitability is required for directors and senior officers. Large banks are subject to incentive-based compensation rules. Institutions must prevent abusive practices, and conduct risk is a supervisory priority. Institutions must identify customers, monitor transactions, and report suspicious activity as part of their AML programs. U.S. sanctions regimes prohibit transactions involving sanctioned parties. Consumer fraud protection is emphasized in fraud prevention. Market manipulation is prohibited. Employees reporting misconduct are protected, and retaliation is prohibited. Regulators assess resilience, controls, and incident response for IT and cybersecurity risks. U.S. privacy is sectoral, not GDPR-based. Cloud risk management is required for outsourcing. Models must be validated and monitored, and AI is subject to existing model risk rules. Open banking is market-driven but regulated through data protection and consumer consent. Branches and operations require regulatory approval. Payment services are regulated under federal and state law. Credit products are subject to consumer protection and fair lending laws. Investment activities require additional registration. Insurance sales by banks are regulated at state level. Cross-border activities must comply with U.S. and foreign laws. Consumer protection is central to U.S. banking regulation, and no single financial ombudsman exists. Banks must support community development. Large banks must conduct stress testing and plan for resolution. Certain banks must disclose risk information publicly. Regulators conduct ongoing supervision. Technology may support compliance but accountability remains with the institution.

## Regulatory Comparison with Australia

### Common Requirements
Institutions must obtain prior authorization for banking and regulated financial activities, maintain adequate capital, liquidity buffers, and transparent group structures. Boards are responsible for governance, risk, and strategy, while senior management must meet accountability and fitness standards. Compliance with AML/CTF, sanctions, fraud prevention, market abuse, and whistleblower protection is mandatory. Institutions must implement robust technology risk, cybersecurity, operational resilience, and data protection controls. Outsourcing, AI, and open banking activities require governance frameworks. Operational standards apply to banking, payments, and credit services, alongside investor and consumer protection. Enterprise risk management, internal controls, compliance, and audit functions are mandatory, supported by stress testing and recovery planning. Institutions must comply with regulatory reporting, financial statement audits, and public disclosure requirements, cooperate with supervisory reviews, manage regulatory changes, and respond to regulatory communications. Breaches result in enforcement actions, and structural changes or exit require regulatory approval. ESG, climate risk, and crypto-asset frameworks are emerging, with regulators encouraging RegTech and SupTech.

### Comparison with Australia
Australia applies a specific Authorised Deposit-taking Institution (ADI) licensing framework under the Banking Act 1959, with prudential standards such as APS 110 for capital and APS 210 for liquidity. The Financial Accountability Regime Act 2023 mandates the registration of accountable persons, complementing the CPS 510 Governance and CPS 520 Fit and Proper requirements. AML/CTF compliance operates under the AML/CTF Act 2006, and sanctions under the Autonomous Sanctions Act 2011. Data protection adheres to the Privacy Act 1988 and Australian Privacy Principles. Consumer credit is regulated by the National Consumer Credit Protection Act 2009, and wealth management requires holding an Australian Financial Services Licence (AFSL). Complaints handling mandates membership in AFCA. Specific prudential standards like CPS 220 for risk management, CPS 230 for operational risk, and CPS 231 for outsourcing define further controls. Regulatory reporting utilizes the D2A format as per APRA Reporting Standards. Crypto activities must comply with existing financial laws, with APRA providing specific guidance on ESG, climate risk, and RegTech.

## Regulatory Comparison with Singapore

### Common Requirements (As a summary)
Both jurisdictions establish comprehensive regulatory frameworks for banking and financial services, requiring prior authorization for all regulated activities, robust capital and liquidity management, and adherence to corporate governance, senior management accountability, and fit and proper standards. Financial crime controls, including AML/CFT, sanctions compliance, and fraud prevention, are mandatory. Institutions must manage technology, cybersecurity, operational, and data protection risks, alongside specific rules for cloud computing, AI, open banking, and digital financial products like payments and lending. Investor and consumer protection, including fair treatment, complaints handling, and vulnerable customer safeguards, are foundational. Enterprise risk management, internal controls, audit, and stress testing are mandated, culminating in comprehensive regulatory reporting, public disclosures, and recovery and resolution planning. Supervisory reviews, enforcement actions, and structured processes for license withdrawal and M&A are in place, with emerging regulations addressing ESG, climate risk, crypto-assets, and RegTech integration.

### Comparison with Singapore (As a summary)
Singapore's regulatory framework explicitly defines regulated activities by statute and subsidiary legislation, requiring entities to assess and seek clarification regarding their products or services within the regulatory perimeter. Restrictions on substantial shareholders and foreign ownership explicitly safeguard financial stability.
